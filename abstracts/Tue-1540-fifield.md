# Latest Efforts of an International Collaborative Team Studying Differences in Effects for Gamma, E-beam and X-ray Irradiation Applications

**Leo Fifield, Randy Schwarz, Mark Murphy**

- Pacific Northwest National Laboratory, Richland, WA

**Nicolas Ludwig, Florent Kuntz**

- Aerial CRT, Strasbourg, France

---

Market forces are increasingly pushing manufacturers of polymer-based medical products and biopharmaceutical production components to consider alternatives to cobalt-60 gamma-rays and ethylene oxide (EO) for sterilization. This diversification naturally includes the technically mature but underutilized options of X-rays and electron beam (e-beam). Published studies indicate there are data and education gaps that impede such diversification. The Office of Radiological Security (ORS), within the National Nuclear Security Administration, is supporting the efforts of an international group of collaborators led by the Pacific Northwest National Laboratory to fill data, education, and tool gaps that impede increased use of machine alternatives for industrial applications. The group, formed in 2018 and called Team Nablo, has produced 15 peer-reviewed journal articles and 75 conference presentations. Recent Nablo projects include:

- Direct comparison of the effects of e-beam and X-ray to those of gamma irradiation on the chemical extractables of common medical polymers and on the clinical analysis of filled blood collection tubes, tying chemical and functional results to material properties.

- The PENELOPE User-Friendly Fast Interface (PUFFIn) Monte Carlo tool, developed as part of this effort, enables access to dose distribution for non-experts, reducing barriers to use of machine sources.

ORS is also supporting opportunities for increased use of machine sources with a project to improve existing passive and calorimetric dosimetry technologies for beam energies below 300 keV (Low-Energy Electron Beam, LEEB). In this work, mathematical modeling and real-time monitoring technologies are being integrated to enhance process control to ensure reproducibility and efficiency for electron beam irradiation.

Status and findings of these efforts supporting use of alternative radiation technologies will be presented.
