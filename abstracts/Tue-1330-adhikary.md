#From Hydroxyl Radicals to Clustered DNA Lesions: Integrating Track-Structure Physics with Quantitative Radiation Damage Measurements

**Amitava Adhikary**

*Free Radical and Radiation Biology Program, Department of Radiation Oncology;* <br>
*Holden Comprehensive Cancer Center;* <br>
*Carver College of Medicine;* <br>
*Iowa City Veterans Affairs Health Care System, The University of Iowa, Iowa 52242, United States*

---

**Purpose:**

My collaboration with Dr. Miral Dizdaroglu and colleagues integrated experimental and computational evidence on room temperature solution phase hydroxyl radical (•OH)-mediated DNA damage (indirect effect) and high-LET track-structure effects in ion-beam irradiated hydrated DNA (direct-type (direct and quasi-direct) effect), with a goal to establish a quantitative framework linking physical energy deposition to chemically defined DNA-lesions formed via both direct-type and indirect effect consistent with the CIRMS mission.

**Materials and Methods:**

We summarized findings from (i) evaluation of •OH in oxidative DNA damage in solution phase at room temperature (indirect effect)<sup>1</sup>, (ii) combining ESR spectroscopy at low temperature and warming up the same samples to room temperature with GC–MS/MS and LC–MS/MS that led to quantification of trapped radicals at 77 K and products at room temperature in <sup>22</sup>Ne ion-beam-irradiated hydrated DNA including LET-resolved Bragg peak dosimetry and TRIM modeling (direct-type effect)<sup>2</sup>, and (iii) Monte Carlo nanodosimetric simulations of clustered DNA damage as a function of LET and fluence (direct-type effect)<sup>3</sup> (Collaboration with Prof. Rezaee and group at JHU).

**Results:**

•OH reacts rapidly with DNA (2.5 x 10<sup>8</sup> M<sup>-1</sup>s<sup>-1</sup>) in solution at room temperature<sup>4</sup> and contributes broadly to base damage, sugar lesions, strand breaks, and tandem lesions in solution (indirect effect). We used the combination of ESR spectroscopy, LC-MS/MS and GC-MS/MS, to report for the first time the formation of DNA products in ion-beam-irradiated hydrated DNA at 77 K (direct-type effect). However, •OH in the icy portion of the frozen systems does not reach DNA so that the dominant radical damage is through ionization of DNA (direct) including the hydration layer (quasi-direct) itself. Our results show that both DNA-radicals and DNA-base damage products formed via both oxidative and reductive pathways increase along the track until just before the Bragg peak is reached. In addition, the DNA-radical and product yields are reduced at Bragg Peak due to radical recombination. Monte Carlo simulations demonstrated that increasing LET enhances clustered lesion complexity while reducing the number of spatially separated damage sites per unit dose.

**Conclusions:**

DNA damage results from combined direct ionization, quasi-direct hydration-shell effects, and radical-mediated chemistry central for the direct-type effect, with •OH central to lesion formation via indirect effect. Monte-Carlo calculations show that high-LET radiation promotes complex clustered DNA damage governing biological effectiveness. Integration of radical detection, quantitative lesion analysis, dosimetry, and nanometric modeling provides a multi-scale framework advancing CIRMS priorities in quantitative radiation measurement science and predictive radiobiology.
