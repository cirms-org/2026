# Dose uniformity ratio changes due to device movement in radiation sterilization

**Tobias Funk, Daniel Badali** <br>
*Dose Insight, Newark, CA, 94553;* <br>
*Triple Ring Technologies, Newark, CA, 94553*

**Eric Bird** <br>
*Triple Ring Technologies, Newark, CA, 94553*

---

In radiation sterilization, it is desired to irradiate products as uniformly as possible to ensure that sterility is achieved and that no part of the product is overexposed, which could lead to material failures. This is expressed as the Dose Uniformity Ratio (DUR), which is the ratio of maximum and minimum dose. Traditionally, the DUR is determined by measurement requiring an actual product. Virtual Dose Mapping is emerging as a tool to evaluate dose distributions in simulations, opening the door to perform studies before an actual device exists and with many more variations than possible with measurements. To fully investigate the range of DURs in a product, it is important to consider how much the product can move in its packaging and how this affects the DUR. To study this, we have developed a shaking algorithm that moves individual parts according to force profiles and physical constraints such as packaging.

Here we present a case study using Dose Insight’s shaking algorithm and TRAD’s RayXpert<sup>®</sup> software to evaluate how movement of the products alters the DUR. For this study, we acquired packages of blood lancets and performed dose uniformity studies in simulation and experiments. We built a fixture to hold three of the lancets steady and equipped them with film dosimeters. We then studied the empty box with just the three lancets and the filled box. We repeated measurements several times to measure the variation of the DUR.

In simulations, we used the shaking algorithm to simulate various realizations of the filled box and used these as input for virtual dose mapping. We also studied the DUR in the empty box. We find that simulations and experiments agree well. The empty box has a lower DUR than the filled box. We also find that the variation of the DUR in the filled box is significantly higher than in the empty box. This can be explained due to the random nature of the orientation of the lancets leading to higher and lower dose maxima and minima.

Overall, virtual dose mapping is an excellent tool to study DUR of packaged products.
