# Fast Neutron Dosimetry and Intensity Modulated Neutron Therapy (IMNT) at the University of Washington Medical Cyclotron Facility (UWMCF)

**Adrienne Lehnert, Marissa Kranz,  Jie Fu, Jay J Liao, Michael R Folkert, Robert D Stewart**

- Department of Radiation Oncology, University of Washington, Seattle, WA 98195

**Robert S Miyaoka**

- Department of Radiology, University of Washington, Seattle, WA 98195

---

The Clinical Neutron Therapy System (CNTS) at the University of Washington is a cyclotron-based fast neutron therapy (NRT) facility used for palliative and curative treatment of cancers that are considered resistant to lower linear energy transfer (LET) modalities, such as locally-advanced salivary gland malignancies. Our facility utilizes a 50.5 MeV <sup>1</sup>H<sup>+</sup> beam incident on a beryllium target to produce fast neutrons (average 22 MeV, maximum 49 MeV). Neutron fields exiting the isocentric gantry are shaped with a 40-leaf collimator mounted within a treatment head and a collimator capable of full 360° of rotation.

We have treated over 3,500 patients since opening in 1984, and continue to innovate, having introduced intensity modulated neutron therapy (IMNT) in late 2022. This necessitated translational R&D in multiple arenas, including a custom build of a commercially available treatment planning system and a detailed Monte Carlo (MCNP6) model of the treatment head. Both of which were validated against output factor, percent depth dose, and lateral profile measurements using NIST-traceable IC-17 chambers. Also required was a novel patient specific quality assurance (PSQA) system utilizing a one-of-a-kind 2D PET imaging system based on the <sup>12</sup>C(*n*,2*n*)<sup>11</sup>C reaction, as fast neutrons are too damaging to the electronics used in other treatment modalities.

As the only remaining center in the world currently treating patients with fast NRT, our success owes much to the knowledge and foresight of our past and present engineering team. We are a rarity in that everything is “in house” and our comprehensive planned maintenance schedule and meticulous documentation contribute to an impressive average 2% annual downtime. Alongside our primary NRT mission, we produce radioisotopes such as <sup>211</sup>At for use in clinical and preclinical research as part of the US Department of Energy’s Isotope Research and Production University Isotope Network. We also collaborate on translational preclinical research (proton FLASH therapy and neutrons) and conduct radiation effects testing of electronics. While NRT has been a relatively niche treatment, recent advances such as the introduction of IMNT create opportunities to improve the therapeutic ratio and to expand the use of fast neutrons for a wider range of clinical indications.
