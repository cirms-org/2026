# A Modern Evaluation of Maximum Dose Limitations in Radiation Processing

**Michael Christofaro**

- Reveam Inc.

---

The Federal Register (Volume 51, Number 75) Report that established the maximum dose delivered to products via irradiation was published in 1986, and the limits set forth 40 years ago have not been re-evaluated since. The resulting document, 21 CFR 179, is becoming dated, and the industry needs to take a science-based approach to modernizing the limits established within. Comments published in this same Federal Register requested that the dose limit be raised to "allow for some latitude in designing a commercial food irradiator," but the FDA declined to evaluate an increase because, "To do otherwise would waste scarce scientific resources that could be used for more productive purposes."

Today we have proverbial mountains of scientific data, journals, and articles showing the limit cannot only safely be increased, but adverse effects on food don't occur until the product receives several kilogray of dose. The WHO has even reported that food treated up to 10 kGy maintains its “wholesomeness” from a toxicologic point of view stating in report that, “The irradiation of foods up to an overall average dose of 10 kGy introduces no special nutritional or microbiological problems.”

Current processing capabilities have now far exceeded those technologically available in 1986, and the potential treatment options grow by the day. Each treatable product may have a different packaging or arrangement. This multitude of arrangements is a massive hurdle when dose uniformity ratio must be so small to accommodate low maximum dose levels. Strict limits established 40 years ago with minimal scientific backing should not burden the expansion of this budding industry, and a revisitation of the maximum dose limit allows for a safer food chain and more effective radiation processing as a whole.
