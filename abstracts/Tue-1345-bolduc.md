# Development of METREPOL-based triage algorithms for estimating H-ARS severity and radiation dose in a baboon mixed-field radiation model

**David L. Bolduc, William F. Blakely** <br>
*Uniformed Services University of the Health Sciences, Armed Forces Radiobiology Research Institute, Scientific Research Department, Bethesda, MD, USA*

 **Lalitha Kurada** <br>
*Henry M. Jackson Foundation, Rockville, MD*

**Francis Hérodin** <br>
*Effets Biologiques des Rayonnements Département, Institut de Recherche Biomédicale des Armées, Brétigny sur Orge, France.*

---

The use of hematological biomarkers is a well-established approach in providing useful diagnostic information for assessment of hematopoietic acute radiation syndrome (H-ARS) severity and radiation dose estimation. Forty-three baboons were evaluated in a radiation model that underwent either mixed-field total-body (neutron/gamma 5.5), total-body (gamma) or partial-body (gamma) irradiations at doses between 2 to 10 Gy (dose rates 6.25 cGy min and 32 cGy min) (Hérodin et al. 2012, 2014; Valente et al. 2015). H-ARS severity levels determined by an analysis of blood-cell count changes measured up to 200 d after irradiation were used to assess overall H-ARS severity classifications (H-ARS 1 to 2(1.5), 2, 2 to 3 (2.5) or 3). A panel of hematologic biomarkers was measured from blood-cell samples collected at 0 to 200 d after exposure using a standard blood cell counter. The database was used in an initial stepwise logistic regression model-fitting approach, which resulted in the down selection of H-ARS relevant biomarkers for determining METREPOL based Response Categories (RC) of either 0 (≤ 2 H-ARS) or 1 (\>2 H-ARS).

The logistic regression H-ARS severity algorithm consisted of lymphocytes log base 10 (p\< 0.001), neutrophils log base 10 (p\< 0.0001) and platelets squared (p\< 0.0001) with an overall model fit p \< 0.001 (R<sup>2</sup> = 0.7018). The predicted RC representing the H-ARS severity outcome for the 200-day model showed an AUC of 0.945 by ROC curve analysis. The percent of cases correctly classified was 89.84%.

A second algorithm for radiation dose estimation was also developed from the total-body exposures dataset using blood variables measured from 1-3 days post irradiation. In this case, a stepwise model-fitting approach was applied to a multiple regression model for down selection of dose relevant blood biomarkers. The dose estimation model consisted of the variable’s lymphocytes log base 10 day 3 (p\< 0.001) and platelets squared day 3 (p\< 0.0001), which yielded an estimation accuracy of R<sup>2</sup> = 0.6760 (p \< 0.001).

The resultant study supports the proof of concept that H-ARS severity RCs and dose estimation algorithms using standard blood-cell parameters can provide rapid triage following gamma and mixed (neutrons/gammas)-field exposures.
