# Toward in vivo dosimetry with plastic scintillation detectors

**Louis Archambault** <br>
*Department of physics, Université Laval and department of radiation oncology, CHU de Québec – Université Laval, Québec City, QC, G1J 1Z4*

---

In vivo dosimetry holds many promises for guaranteeing accurate dose delivery to radiotherapy patients, especially in the context of complex treatment modalities and hypofractionation. Despite these promises, it is a process that is clinically challenging to implement and not every type of dosimeter is suitable for in vivo dosimetry.

Plastic scintillators have several well established advantages that could serve in vivo dosimetry well: water equivalence, dose and energy linearity, fast emission. However, PSDs have other potential limitations such as the presence of an optical cable and a small temperature dependence. Our goal is to present an overview of how plastic scintillation dosimeters (PSDs) can be used for in vivo applications and to present recent advances that facilitate the use of these dosimeters for online monitoring of delivered doses.

After reviewing the current state of PSD dosimeters and the different detectors commercially available, we will show how they can be successfully used in a wide range of situations from preclinical studies to tracking dose to cardiac implanted electronic devices. We will provide examples of how PSD-based in vivo dosimetry can effectively detect treatment deviations. Finally we will focus on how small PSD arrays composed of 2 to 7 probes can be used to increase confidence in treatment accuracy.
